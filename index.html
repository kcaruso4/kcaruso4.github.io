A view of global educational disparities along the lines of gender, urban/rural divide, and wealth

Over the last 200 years, the number of children attending primary school globally has grown from 2.3 million to 700 million today, covering nearly 90 percent of the world’s school-age children[1]. However, despite these massive overall increases, progress has in some cases not been entirely equitable, and in other cases perpetuated existing inequalities. This website’s purpose is to document and visualize global educational disparities, not only between countries, but also within countries along the lines of gender, urban versus rural housing, and wealth. For each of these categories, we visualize to measures of educational progress: (1) secondary school completion rates (percentage of young people aged 15-24 years who have completed lower secondary school) and (2) lower secondary school reading achievement (percentage of youth of lower secondary school age who take part in a reading assessment passing four levels of increasing difficulty). These measures were obtained from UNESCO [2]. Though certainly not comprehensive, looking at both completion rates and learning achievements offers a multidimensional perspective into educational inequalities that emphasizes the interconnected roles of opportunities and outcomes. Our belief is that advancing education for all requires an honest look at what inequalities in education exist today. We hope that this website can serve as a first step in such an honest reckoning.

Wealth

Most of this increase in average educational attainment has been heavily skewed towards rich countries. In fact, the Brookings Institution estimates that “without a fundamental rethinking of current approaches to education, it’s going to take another 100 years for children in developing countries to reach the education levels achieved in developed countries.” Inequalities in educational opportunities and learning accomplishments stemming from wealth are also certainly not limited to between countries, but within countries as well. Sociology Professor Fabian T. Pfeffer of the University of Michigan argues just that in his paper Growing Wealth Gaps in Education: “despite overall growth in educational attainment and some signs of decreasing wealth gaps in high school attainment and college access,” he finds “a large and rapidly increasing wealth gap in college attainment between cohorts born in the 1970s and 1980s, respectively” in the U.S. This stark educational inequality will only continue to unfairly limit a poor student’s overall potential compared with their richer counterparts, abroad or domestic, and without access to the same opportunities, this gap seems only destined to grow.
